Examined Sanskrit pedagogy: stem-grouped versus sequential tense instruction methods

Yes, लट् = present. And no — they're not taught all at once. They're taught in stem groups, which is way more efficient than it looks.

Of your 5 लकार, four (लट्, लङ्, लोट्, विधिलिङ्) sit on the same present stem. Only लृट् uses a different stem (the -स्य/-ष्य infix). So once you've nailed the 3×3 for √कृ in लट्, the other three are nearly free — same skeleton, just swap personal endings and add a marker.

Traditional पाठशाला method: memorize the full 9-cell रूपावली aloud for one लकार, then build all लकार for that single धातु before moving to the next root. This is धातुरूप memorization — complete paradigm tables, chanted. By the time you've done √कृ, √भू, √गम्, √अस्, you've internalized hundreds of forms but only learned 4 roots.

Modern textbooks (Deshpande, Goldman, Maurer): stage by stem-system. लट् first, then लोट्/विधिलिङ्/लङ् in subsequent chapters because they share the stem, then लृट् as a new stem-system, then लिट् (perfect) last because reduplication is its own beast.

For Gita-reading specifically, the efficient sequence is:

  1. लट् — establish 3×3 + गण-specific stem
  2. लोट् + विधिलिङ् together — same stem, swap endings (Krishna's commands + the rhetorical "should")
  3. लङ् — same stem + अ- augment + secondary endings (the narrative spine: उवाच, अब्रवीत्)
  4. लृट् — separate -स्य- stem (Krishna's prophecies, "you will become")

So really it's 1 stem-learning effort for 4 लकार, plus 1 more for लृट्. The 45-form total is less daunting than it sounds.

Claude responded: Two voice-like sets of personal endings a root takes:

Two voice-like sets of personal endings a root takes:

  • परस्मैपद ("word for another") — action's fruit goes to someone else. Default/transitive feel. Endings: -ति, -सि, -मि...
  • आत्मनेपद ("word for oneself") — action's fruit returns to the agent. Reflexive/middle feel. Endings: -ते, -से, -ए...

Each root is lexically marked as one of three:

  • परस्मैपदी — only P endings (√भू, √गम्, √पठ्)
  • आत्मनेपदी — only Ā endings (√लभ्, √सेव्, √मन्)
  • उभयपदी — takes both, sometimes with meaning shift (√कृ, √यज्, √नी)

Quick spot in Gita: उवाच (P, "he said") vs मन्यसे (Ā, "you think [to yourself]"). The -ते/-से tail is the Ā tell.

Practical note: the 3×3 grid I gave you doubles to 3×3×2 = 18 per लकार if a root is उभयपदी. But in practice you learn whichever पद the root takes, so it stays 9.

Claude responded: Three honest answers, in order of how often they actually apply:

Three honest answers, in order of how often they actually apply:

1. Most of the time, you don't choose — the root chooses for you. 99% of the time, पद is lexical. √भू is परस्मैपदी, full stop. You don't get to say भवते. √लभ् is आत्मनेपदी; अलभत् is wrong, अलभत is right. So the "when to use" question collapses into "memorize which पद each root takes" — same as memorizing der/die/das in German. The classical grammarians built diagnostic rules (Pāṇini 1.3.12–77) but for a reader, it's vocabulary.

2. For उभयपदी roots, classical rules assign पद by semantic/syntactic context. Pāṇini's rules trigger on specific conditions. A few that actually show up:

  • √कृ: परस्मैपद when fruit goes to another (देवदत्तः ओदनं पचति "cooks for someone"), आत्मनेपद when fruit returns to agent (देवदत्तः ओदनं पचते "cooks for himself"). This is the textbook case (P 1.3.72, स्वरितञितः कर्त्रभिप्राये क्रियाफले).
  • √यज्: P when the priest sacrifices for a patron (यजति), Ā when one sacrifices for oneself (यजते).
  • Voice-shift: passive and middle-ish constructions force Ā regardless of root (क्रियते "is done").

3. In post-Vedic/Epic Sanskrit (including the Gita), the distinction is already eroding. By the time of the Mahābhārata, poets pick पद partly for meter — पठति vs पठते both mean "reads," but one fits the pāda and the other doesn't. Classical poetry exploits this freely. So in the Gita you'll see आत्मनेपद forms where strict Pāṇinian rules would want परस्मैपद, and vice versa. Don't over-interpret meaning from पद choice in śloka — it's often metrical.

Practical Gita-reading rule: when you hit a verb, identify पद to nail down the ending set (so you parse person/number correctly). Don't try to extract reflexive meaning from आत्मनेपद forms — by Gita-era usage, that semantic load is mostly gone. The form tells you who and when; the meaning comes from context.

गण = conjugation class. Sanskrit's 2000+ roots are sorted into 10 classes based on how the present stem is formed from the root. Same root family, same stem-formation rule, same conjugation pattern.

Critical point: गण only matters for the present system (लट्, लङ्, लोट्, विधिलिङ्). For लृट् (future), लिट् (perfect), and most derivations, गण is irrelevant — they all use different stem-formation rules that ignore गण.

These are what trip up readers.

For the Gita: most verbs you'll hit are गण 1, 4, 6, 10. The athematic exceptions you cannot avoid are √अस् (be, गण 2), √इ (go, गण 2), √हन् (kill, गण 2), √ज्ञा (know, गण 9), √शृ/शृणु (hear, गण 5), √कृ (do, गण 8). Sanskrit Verb Formation — The Full Stack

A finite Sanskrit verb is built in layers. Each layer answers one question.

The 5 layers

1. धातु (root) — the lexical core

  • ~2000 exist; ~192 cover 90% of literature
  • Carries meaning + two fixed properties: गण and पद

2. गण (class, 1–10) — how to build the present stem

  • Only matters for the present system (लट्, लङ्, लोट्, विधिलिङ्)
  • Determines what gets added/changed to turn root → stem
  • Example: √भू (गण 1) → भव-, √तुद् (गण 6) → तुद-, √क्री (गण 9) → क्रीणा-

3. लकार (tense/mood, 10 total — 5 you need) — which stem and which endings

  • लट् present, लङ् past, लोट् imperative, विधिलिङ् optative → all use the present stem (गण-dependent)
  • लृट् future → uses future stem (root + स्य/ष्य, गण irrelevant)
  • Each लकार has its own ending set (primary, secondary, imperative, optative)

4. पद (P / Ā) — which family of endings within that लकार

  • Lexically fixed per root: परस्मैपदी, आत्मनेपदी, or उभयपदी
  • P: -ति, -सि, -मि / Ā: -ते, -से, -ए
  • Tells you which 9-cell ending grid to pull from

5. पुरुष × वचन (3 × 3 = 9) — which cell to pick

  • प्रथम/मध्यम/उत्तम × एक/द्वि/बहु

The build pipeline

धातु
 └─ गण rule → present stem (or +स्य → future stem)
     └─ लकार → selects ending set + augment/marker
         └─ पद → selects P or Ā column of that set
             └─ पुरुष × वचन → picks the exact ending
                 = finite verb form

Worked example: "you (sg.) will do" from √कृ

  1. धातु: √कृ (do/make), गण 8, उभयपदी
  2. लकार: लृट् (future) → use future stem, not present
  3. Future stem: कृ + ष्य = करिष्य- (with vowel changes)
  4. पद: pick P (you're addressing Arjuna actively, say)
  5. पुरुष × वचन: मध्यम एकवचन → ending -सि
  6. Result: करिष्यसि ("you will do")

Same root, same person/number, but लट् मध्यम एक P = करोषि ("you do"). Same root, लङ् प्रथम एक P = अकरोत् ("he did"). Same root, लोट् मध्यम एक P = कुरु ("do!"). The 5-layer stack is what swaps under you.

What the Khoomeik chart gives you per root

  • गण ✓ (color)
  • Meaning ✓
  • 3sg present form ✓ (which encodes पद + present stem in one shot)

What you still need to learn separately

  • Suppletive present stems (दृश् → पश्य-, गम् → गच्छ-, स्था → तिष्ठ-) — chart shows them but doesn't explain
  • लिट् (perfect) forms — reduplicated, often irregular (उवाच, चकार, बभूव). Recognition-only is fine.
  • Non-finite derivations layered on roots: participles, absolutives (-त्वा), infinitives (-तुम्), causatives (-अय-), passives (-य-)

The mental model

Root tells you what. गण tells you how to build the present stem. लकार tells you when/why and switches stems for non-present tenses. पद tells you which ending family. Person×number picks the cell.

Adjectives — they're just nouns wearing matching clothes

Sanskrit has no separate adjective declension system. An adjective uses the same case-ending tables you already know (राम-style for masculine -अ stems, फल-style for neuter -अ stems, रमा-style for feminine -आ stems, etc.).

The rule is विशेषण-विशेष्य भाव / सामानाधिकरण्य: the adjective agrees with the noun it modifies in all three of:

  1. विभक्ति (case)
  2. वचन (number)
  3. लिंग (gender)

You already fought this battle in Gita 1.1 (समवेताः युयुत्सवः — both प्रथमा बहु पुल्लिंग) and 2.5 (महानुभावान् गुरून् — both द्वितीया बहु पुल्लिंग). That's the entire system.

The wrinkle: an adjective stem like शुद्ध ("pure") doesn't belong to one gender — it shape-shifts:

So "learning adjectives" = learning the same declension tables and trusting the agreement rule. There's no new memorization beyond what the noun system already gave you.

Comparison degrees (positive / comparative / superlative) do add suffixes:

  • -तर (comparative): शुद्ध → शुद्धतर ("purer")
  • -तम (superlative): शुद्ध → शुद्धतम ("purest")
  • Irregular set: श्रेष्ठ (best), ज्यायस् (greater), कनिष्ठ (youngest) — recognition pack, not generative

These suffixed forms then decline like normal adjectives. So +1 on top of what you have.

Pronominal adjectives (सर्व, अन्य, एक, कतम, यत्, तत्, एतत्, इदम्, अदस्) follow a slightly different declension — the सर्वनाम pattern. Differences are small (e.g., सर्वस्मै instead of सर्वाय in dative). Worth a separate one-page reference; not a separate system.

Adverbs — almost no system at all

This is genuinely simpler. Sanskrit adverbs are mostly:

1. Frozen case forms (the most common pattern)

  • Neuter accusative singular of an adjective often functions adverbially:
    • शीघ्रम् "quickly" (from शीघ्र "quick")
    • सत्यम् "truly"
    • सुखम् "happily"
  • Instrumental singular for manner:
    • सुखेन "with ease / happily"
    • बलात् (ablative) "by force / forcibly"
  • Locative for time/place:
    • प्रातः "in the morning"

So if you can decline a noun, you can already produce most adverbs. They're not a new word class — they're nouns/adjectives stuck in a particular case, used adverbially.

2. Suffix -तस् ("from / -ly")

  • मूलतः "from the root / fundamentally"
  • सर्वतः "from all sides / entirely"
  • शास्त्रतः "according to scripture"

Pronouns deserve their own treatment — they're a real system, not a ride-along. Closed class, but high frequency and mildly irregular declension. The Gita is dense with them.

The system

Sanskrit pronouns split into two groups by behavior:

1. Personal pronouns (अस्मद् "I/we", युष्मद् "you") — wildly irregular, suppletive (different stems for different cases), no gender distinction. Pure memorization.

2. सर्वनाम pronouns (everything else: सः, एषः, अयम्, यः, कः, सर्वः, अन्यः...) — decline like adjectives but with a few systematic deviations from the राम pattern. Learn the deltas, not new tables.

Personal pronouns — pure memorization

No gender (same form for he/she). Three numbers. Suppletive = the stem itself changes between cases — अहम् / माम् / मया / मम have nothing visually in common. You can't generate them; you memorize them.

The यद्...तद् correlative — the Gita's signature move

Sanskrit doesn't say "the man who came is my brother." It says "who man came, that (man) my brother":

यः नरः आगच्छत्, सः मम भ्राता।

The यद्-clause sets up a referent; the तद्-clause picks it up. Both pronouns agree in gender/number with their respective noun, but their case is independent — determined by each clause's own grammar.

The Gita uses this constantly:

यः शास्त्रविधिमुत्सृज्य वर्तते कामकारतः, न सः सिद्धिमवाप्नोति (16.23) "He who abandons scripture's rule and acts from desire, he does not attain perfection."

Other correlative pairs:

  • यदा...तदा (when...then)
  • यत्र...तत्र (where...there)
  • यथा...तथा (as...so)
  • यावत्...तावत् (as long as...so long)
  • यदि...तर्हि / तदा (if...then)

This is the syntactic backbone of philosophical Sanskrit. Half of Krishna's teachings are यद्...तद् structures.

Reflexive — स्व and आत्मन्

  • स्व ("own") declines like सर्व, used as a possessive: स्वं धर्मम् ("one's own duty")
  • आत्मन् ("self") declines as a masculine -न् stem (आत्मा / आत्मानम् / आत्मना / आत्मने / आत्मनः / आत्मनि), used reflexively and as the philosophical "Self." Gita uses both senses constantly — sometimes ambiguously, deliberately.

आत्मना आत्मानम् उद्धरेत् (6.5 — "one should lift the self by the self")

1. समास (Compounds) — the biggest gap

This is the largest missing piece. Sanskrit compounds words aggressively. The Gita is full of them. धर्मक्षेत्रे कुरुक्षेत्रे in 1.1 — both are compounds (धर्म + क्षेत्र, कुरु + क्षेत्र). हृदयदौर्बल्यम् in 2.3 (हृदय + दौर्बल्य). मधुसूदन, परन्तप, अरिसूदन — Krishna's epithets are all compounds.

Six classical types:

  • तत्पुरुष — case-based (राजपुत्रः = "king's son", a 6th-case relationship between members)
  • कर्मधारय — adjective + noun, both same case (नीलोत्पलम् = "blue lotus")
  • द्वन्द्व — list, "X and Y" (रामलक्ष्मणौ = "Rāma and Lakṣmaṇa")
  • बहुव्रीहि — possessive, "[one] having X" (पीताम्बरः = "yellow-robed [one]" = Krishna)
  • अव्ययीभाव — adverbial (यथाशक्ति = "according to ability")
  • द्विगु — numerical (पञ्चवटी = "group of five trees")

विग्रह = the un-compounding step. Like पदच्छेद but for inside compound words. The decode pipeline really should be: पदच्छेद → विग्रह → अन्वय → translation.

This is a real sub-app candidate. Compounds are where intermediate Sanskrit gets hard, and recognizing the type changes the meaning entirely (पीताम्बरः as तत्पुरुष = "yellow garment"; as बहुव्रीहि = "the one wearing yellow garments" = Krishna).

2. कारक — the semantic role layer above विभक्ति

विभक्ति is the morphological case (the ending). कारक is the semantic role the noun plays in the action. They overlap but aren't identical:

  • कर्ता (agent) — usually प्रथमा, but तृतीया in passive
  • कर्म (patient/object) — usually द्वितीया, but प्रथमा in passive
  • करण (instrument) — तृतीया
  • सम्प्रदान (recipient) — चतुर्थी
  • अपादान (source) — पञ्चमी
  • अधिकरण (location) — सप्तमी

Pāṇini built the whole grammar around कारक, not विभक्ति. The reason this matters: passive constructions (which the Gita uses constantly) flip विभक्ति assignments — the agent moves from प्रथमा to तृतीया, the patient from द्वितीया to प्रथमा, but the कारक roles stay the same. Without this layer, passive sentences look broken.

रामेण रावणः हतः ("by Rāma, Rāvaṇa was killed") — रामेण is तृतीया but कर्ता; रावणः is प्रथमा but कर्म.

This is conceptual, not a new memorization layer. But it's missing from what we've discussed. Should be a one-page explainer in the noun reference.

Articles (a / an / the) — don't exist

Sanskrit has no articles. None. There is no word for "a" or "the".

  • रामः = "Rāma" / "a Rāma" / "the Rāma" — context decides
  • पुरुषः = "a man" or "the man"

Definiteness is conveyed (when it matters) by:

  • Demonstratives: सः पुरुषः ("that man" → effectively "the man")
  • एषः / अयम्: ("this man" → contextually definite)
  • Word order or context: usually just left to inference

This is actually a relief — one less word class to track. Hindi inherited this: यह आदमी / वह आदमी do the work English's "the" does. Sanskrit verses never need an article because the case ending + context already pins down the referent.

Prepositions — also don't exist as a separate class

English needs prepositions because English nouns don't change form. "to the king," "from the king," "with the king," "in the king" — the noun "king" is identical; the preposition carries the relationship.

Sanskrit nouns do change form. The विभक्ति (case ending) carries the relationship.

What's missing (from a European-grammar-checklist perspective)

The English/Hindi learner's instinct is to look for:

  • ❌ Articles → don't exist

  • ✅ Postpositions → small set, govern specific cases

  1. उपसर्ग — verbal prefixes, 22 of them, hugely productive

Good question. The distinction is fundamental and once you see it, English splits along the same line — just less dramatically.

The core distinction

Declinable / inflected = the word changes its form to express grammatical relationships (case, number, gender, person, tense).

Indeclinable / uninflected = the word never changes, no matter where it appears in the sentence.

In Sanskrit:

  • रामः, रामम्, रामेण, रामाय, रामात्, रामस्य, रामे — same word (राम), 7 different forms
  • च = च = च = च — always च, everywhere, forever

Sanskrit calls the indeclinable category अव्यय — literally "non-changing" (अ- + व्यय "expense/change"). Pāṇini's elegant split is:

सुप्तिङन्तं पदम् — "a पद (word) ends in either सुप् (case ending) or तिङ् (verb ending)."

Plus अव्यय, which by definition has neither. So every word in Sanskrit is one of three things:

  1. सुबन्त — ending in a case ending (nouns, pronouns, adjectives) → declinable
  2. तिङन्त — ending in a verbal ending (finite verbs) → conjugable
  3. अव्यय — ending in nothing that changes → indeclinable

English has the same split, but most words are barely declinable

English looks like it doesn't decline because the inflection system collapsed centuries ago, but it's still there — just impoverished:

Declinable in English:

  • Nouns: cat / cats / cat**'s** / cats' — that's it. 4 forms, mostly differing by an -s. Compare Sanskrit's 24 forms per noun (8 cases × 3 numbers).
  • Pronouns: still genuinely declined. I / me / my / mine, he / him / his, who / whom / whose. This is the last fortress of English case marking.
  • Verbs: walk / walks / walked / walking — 4 forms. Compare Sanskrit's 90+ finite forms per verb (5 लकार × 9 cells × 2 पद).
  • Adjectives: tall / taller / tallest — 3 forms, only for comparison. No agreement with noun (English doesn't say talls men).

Indeclinable in English (the "अव्यय" of English):

  • Prepositions: of, to, in, on, by, with, from — never change. "of the cat," "of the cats," "of him" — of stays of.
  • Conjunctions: and, but, or, because, although, if, while
  • Articles: a, an, the (technically "an" is a phonological variant of "a," not an inflection)
  • Interjections: oh, ah, alas, wow
  • Many adverbs: very, quite, soon, often, here, there, now

So English's word classes split roughly:

The parallel that makes it click

Look at the Sanskrit अव्यय list and the English indeclinable list side by side:

Sanskrit अव्ययEnglish equivalentBoth indeclinable?

These are the same words, doing the same syntactic work, in both languages — and neither language inflects them. It's not coincidence. There's something about the function these words perform (connectives, modifiers of whole sentences, exclamations, fixed deictic references) that resists inflection in most languages. They're already abstract enough that case/number/tense doesn't apply.

Why the contrast is so sharp in Sanskrit and so muted in English

Sanskrit's declension system is dense. A noun has 24 forms; a verb has 90+. So when a word doesn't inflect, it stands out clearly — it's an obvious member of a different category.

English's declension system is shrunken. A noun has ~4 forms; a verb has ~4 forms. So the "declinable / indeclinable" line is much fainter — most English words look indeclinable because they barely inflect even when they technically can.

This is why English speakers don't intuitively feel the declinable/indeclinable distinction: in English, "cat" and "and" both look pretty much the same most of the time. In Sanskrit, राम wears 24 different costumes while च wears nothing — the contrast is unmissable.

A useful diagnostic

If you can answer "what's the plural of this word?" or "what's the past tense of this word?" with a different form, it's declinable/conjugable. If the answer is "it doesn't have one" or "the same word," it's indeclinable.

  • cat → cats ✓ declinable
  • and → ands? ✗ indeclinable
  • walk → walked ✓ conjugable
  • very → veried? ✗ indeclinable

Same diagnostic in Sanskrit:

  • राम → रामौ ✓ declinable
  • च → चौ? ✗ indeclinable (अव्यय)
  • भू → भवति ✓ conjugable
  • एव → एवति? ✗ indeclinable

The conceptual upshot

English speakers often think of grammar as "rules for changing word endings." The indeclinable category is the reminder that a huge fraction of any language is words that simply are, with no ending mechanics at all — they exist to glue, modify, point, exclaim, or connect. They carry meaning structurally rather than morphologically.

Sanskrit's grammatical tradition foregrounds this category clearly (अव्यय is one of three top-level word types). English's doesn't — there's no familiar single English term meaning "indeclinable word." The closest is "function word" or "particle," but neither is in everyday use.

So when you encounter "अव्यय" in Sanskrit grammar, the mental translation is: "This word is like English 'and' or 'because' or 'now' — it does its job by sitting there, not by changing shape."

Validated intuition through syntactic hierarchy and mood analysis

Your intuition is exactly right. कुरुष्व is the structural anchor. Three converging reasons:

1. The यद्...तद् correlative structure — the same one we just covered under pronouns.

The five यत्-clauses are subordinate; they set up referents:

  • यत् करोषि — "whatever you do"
  • यत् अश्नासि — "whatever you eat"
  • यत् जुहोषि — "whatever you offer in sacrifice"
  • यत् ददासि — "whatever you give"
  • यत् तपस्यसि — "whatever austerity you perform"

The single तद्-clause picks them all up: तत् कुरुष्व मदर्पणम् — "that, do as an offering to Me."

By Sanskrit syntax, the तद्-clause carries the main predicate; the यद्-clauses are dependent. Among six finite verbs, exactly one sits in the main clause — कुरुष्व.

2. Mood — imperative vs indicative.

Five verbs are लट् (present indicative) — descriptions of what Arjuna already does. कुरुष्व alone is लोट् (imperative) — Krishna's actual command. The यत्-clauses are what already happens; कुरुष्व is what to do with it. Mood marks the hierarchy.

3. √कृ is the meta-verb.

Krishna picks √कृ for the imperative deliberately. "Eat, offer, give, perform austerity" all reduce to "doing things" — √कृ is general enough to encompass all five. The same root that appears in one of the यत्-clauses (करोषि) also serves as the cover-imperative for all of them. The verse is self-referential: "whatever you do... do that as an offering."

On कुरुष्व vs कुरु specifically:

कुरु would be the परस्मैपद imperative; कुरुष्व is आत्मनेपद. Meter forces कुरुष्व here — अनुष्टुभ् needs the syllable count, and कुरु would leave the pāda short. But traditional commentators read the आत्मनेपद as semantically apt: the offering returns to the agent, bhakti benefits the bhakta. Whether the choice is primarily metrical or doctrinal is debatable; both readings are grammatically present.

The takeaway — and this is your parent project's anchor principle in action:

"Finite verb = sentence anchor. Everything else orbits it."

Six finite verbs in this verse, but the structural test (which clause type, which mood) reveals only one true anchor. The other five are inputs; कुरुष्व is the operator that transforms them all into bhakti.
